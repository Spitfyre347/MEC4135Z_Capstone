\subsection{Tensile Tests}
Starting with the tensile tests for the material states, \textbf{ASTM E8/8M} was selected as the most appropriate standard to adhere to for testing. As per the outline from \textit{ASTM.org} \cite{ASTME8}, this standard governs the tension testing of metallic materials, and fits the consultants' protocol descriptions.

For each sample, shown in the table below, 8 repeat tensile tests were performed.  From this point on, each sample will be referred to using just the first letter and last letters of their designations after the hyphen (i.e. ``\textit{W-HT2}" for ``\textit{WR718-HT2}").

\subsubsection{Excluded Tests}

Of these tests, several need be excluded, which we'll outline below. For \textit{A-HM}, \textbf{Test 7} is excluded as its strain rate of $10mm/min$ deviates from the otherwise-standard rate of $1mm/min$. Similarly, we exclude \textbf{Test 6} for \textit{A-HT1} and \textbf{Test 1} for \textit{W-HT2}, both for the same reason. 

One test also included measurements made with a ruler rather than a caliper, and thus needs to be excluded. This is \textbf{Test 4} for \textit{A-HT1}.

In terms of issues preceding/during testing, we exclude \textit{A-HM} \textbf{Test 2} since the specimen had a rough surface on the gauge region prior to testing, and \textbf{Test 5} since the extensometer was not calibrated, and for the same reason, \textbf{Test 7} for \textit{A-HT2}. For \textit{A-HT1}, we exclude \textbf{Test 1} since the specimen was not clamped vertically between the grips during testing. For \textit{A-HT2}, we exclude \textbf{Test 4}, since the specimen fracture occurred outside of the gauge region, and \textbf{Test 7} of \textit{W-HT2} for the same reason. 

Finally, two tests used a crosshead instead of a more-accurate extensometer to measure displacement, namely \textbf{Test 3} for \textit{A-HT2} and \textbf{Test 4} for \textit{W-HT2}. We exclude these as well.

\subsubsection{Included Tests}
With the exclusions above, we retain exactly 5 tests for each sample, and thus \textbf{we will use 6 repeat tests} for each sample in our analysis. This is done to utilize the fact that every sample had precisely 3 invalid tests, and 5 valid ones, giving us a large enough sample size to perform an accurate and precise analysis. To exclude any tests, such as only considering 4 for each sample, would be to needlessly remove valid data for no reason, and would generally decrease the quality of our analysis. As such, we use the maximum number of test runs available to us. This includes tests \textbf{1, 3, 4, 6, 8} for \textit{A-HM}, \textbf{2, 3, 5, 7, 8} for \textit{A-HT1}, \textbf{1, 2, 5, 6, 8} for \textit{A-HT2} and \textbf{2, 3, 5, 6, 7} for \textit{W-HT2}. 


\subsubsection{Statistical Decisions}
To arrive at the results shown in the section below, a number of statistical decisions were made with regards to the reporting and representation of data. The most obvious of these is the test exclusions above, however with the tests narrowed down, several additional decisions were made that need be mentioned. Additional graphs, such as those aiding in calculations, are given in the appendix, so as to not clutter the other, more-important graphical results. All non-additional stress strain curves, specifically those which showcase the multiple tests runs, are shown in portrait orientations, so as to display any deviations in values which may have occurred within the runs. Landscape displays of these graphs would show the curves as a single thick line, making them less informative overall. Finally, bar graphs were chosen to compare various properties of the heat treatments, and, in particular, yield strength and ultimate tensile strength were chosen to be displayed on the same graph, so as to show comparisons between values as well, i.e. for a single heat treatment.

\subsection{Tensile Tests}
For our tensile tests, we may obtain \textbf{engineering stress/strain curves} for each of our 4 samples, shown on the next page.

Following this, we may, too, compute \textbf{true stress/strain curves} for the 4 samples, shown on the page following on (i.e. page X). We utilize the two equations shown below:

$$\sigma_{true} = \sigma_{eng}(1+\varepsilon_{eng})\quad \quad \quad \quad \varepsilon_{true} = \ln(1+\varepsilon_{eng}) $$

Thereafter, we may begin arriving at several more results through computation. We begin by determining the \textbf{coefficients of strength and strain hardening}. Using the equation below, which applies to the region of \textbf{plastic deformation} for our graph, we may compute these values as, respectively, the intercept and gradient of a logarithmic true stress/strain curve (specific to the plastic region).

$$\ln \sigma_{true} = \ln K + n \ln {\varepsilon_t} $$

We begin by averaging our true stress/strain curves (Figs. \ref{fig:tss1}-\ref{fig:tss4}) across all 5 tests to obtain a single curve which we will use to compute our coefficients. We then plot subfunctions of this true stress/strain curve for each sample, using a log-log scale (in particular, $\ln-\ln$), and specific to the plastic region. These are shown in the appendix (Section 7, Figs. \ref{fig:ltss1}-\ref{fig:ltss4}), and we obtain our desired linearity and arrive at the following results for each sample.

Moving on, we may also compute the \textbf{yield strength, $\sigma_{ys}$, ultimate tensile strength, $\sigma_{UTS}$, elongation to failure, $\varepsilon_{fail}$} and \textbf{Young's modulus} for each heat treatment. To calculate these, we begin by averaging our engineering stress/strain values for each sample, to obtain a single curve. Thereafter, we plot a subfunction of each of these curves, specifically limited to the elastic region, so as to compute \textbf{elastic} (i.e. Young's) \textbf{modulus} and the yield strength, which are, respectively, the gradient and $y_{max}$. These plots are given in the appendix, as with those prior (Section 7, Figs. \ref{fig:ess1}-\ref{fig:ess4})

The other values are more straightforward, with $\sigma_{UTS}$ and $\varepsilon_{fail}$ given by our $y$ and $x$ maxima, respectively (of the entire stress/strain curve). We arrive at the table below.
